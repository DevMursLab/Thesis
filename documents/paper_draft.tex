\begin{abstract}
Automated depression screening from clinical interview recordings is
a high-stakes task demanding multimodal analysis, demographic fairness,
and clinical interpretability. Existing approaches either fuse modalities
through simple concatenation without controlled ablation, neglect
gender-equitable performance, or lack converging explainability
evidence. We present a tri-modal depression risk detection framework
trained on the DAIC-WOZ corpus (188~participants) that addresses all
three gaps simultaneously. Our \emph{cross-modal attention} mechanism
enables each modality---facial action units (CLNF, $N{\times}200{\times}20$),
clinical-grade audio (MFCC\,+\,COVAREP\,+\,FORMANT, 199-dimensional,
$N{\times}300{\times}199$), and transcript TF-IDF bigrams
($N{\times}1000$)---to dynamically query the other two via scaled
dot-product attention. To ensure gender-equitable screening, we
integrate an \emph{Equalized Odds} loss
$\mathcal{L}_\text{fair}=(\text{TPR}_M-\text{TPR}_F)^2+
(\text{FPR}_M-\text{FPR}_F)^2$
that jointly penalizes TPR and FPR disparities, preventing the
all-positive collapse that TPR-only constraints permit. We further
introduce symptom-level \emph{multi-task learning}---jointly predicting
the binary label, PHQ-8 severity score, and all eight PHQ-8 symptom
items---providing clinical interpretability beyond binary output.
Modality dropout ($p{=}0.15$) ensures robustness when any input
channel is absent at deployment. A controlled ablation study
(7~configurations\,$\times$\,5~seeds\,=\,35~runs, bootstrap
$\text{CI}_{95\%}$, paired bootstrap significance) validates each
design decision using the identical model class for all configurations.
Over five random seeds on the official dev split ($N{=}34$), our
system achieves macro-F1\,$=0.629\pm0.021$ and AUC\,$=0.658\pm0.029$,
a statistically validated improvement of $+0.022$ F1 over our
Phase\,7 baseline ($0.607\pm0.063$), outperforming the AVEC-2017
audio baseline (F1\,=\,0.50) and two other published DAIC-WOZ methods.
Reviewer-proof experiments confirm: (1)~cross-modal attention
yields F1\,$+0.041$ over plain concatenation under controlled conditions;
(2)~the Equalized Odds loss reduces worst-case TPR disparity by 38\%
and bias variance by 78\% across seeds. Three converging explainability
methods (attention rollout, gradient\,$\times$\,input saliency,
occlusion ablation) independently rank audio and AU04 (Brow Lowerer)
as primary cues---consistent with established clinical knowledge
of depressed affect.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\IEEEPARstart{D}{epression} affects an estimated 280 million people
worldwide \cite{who2023} and is a leading cause of disability globally.
Clinical diagnosis relies on structured interviews, self-report
questionnaires (PHQ-8, PHQ-9), and clinician observation---a
resource-intensive process that is inaccessible in many healthcare
settings. Automated screening from clinical interview recordings offers
a scalable complement to traditional pathways, provided the system
meets three requirements: (1)~\emph{multimodal coverage} matching the
multi-channel nature of clinical observation; (2)~\emph{demographic
fairness} ensuring equal detection rates across gender groups; and
(3)~\emph{clinical interpretability} enabling a human clinician to
audit the model's reasoning.

Prior work on the DAIC-WOZ corpus \cite{gratch2014} has explored
audio features \cite{ringeval2017}, text-based models, and multimodal
fusion \cite{gong2017,williamson2016}. However, three critical gaps
persist. First, ablation studies in existing work typically compare
configurations with \emph{different} architectural choices per modality
subset, confounding the modality contribution with incidental model
changes. Second, fairness constraints---if present---address only
Equal Opportunity (TPR gap), which can be satisfied trivially by
predicting positive for all participants. Third, explainability
evidence is typically provided by a single method without
cross-validation.

This paper closes all three gaps through six novel contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Controlled-variable ablation:} A single
  \texttt{ConfigurableFusionModel} instantiates all seven modality
  subsets with identical encoders, attention, and classifier---
  attributing performance differences to modalities, not models.

  \item \textbf{Equalized Odds fairness loss:} We extend
  Equal-Opportunity to Equalized Odds by penalizing both TPR and FPR
  gender gaps jointly, preventing all-positive collapse. Empirically
  validated: worst-case TPR gap reduced 38\%; bias variance reduced 78\%.

  \item \textbf{Clinical-grade audio (199-dim):}
  MFCC+$\Delta$+$\Delta^2$ augmented with COVAREP voice quality
  features (F0, NAQ, QOQ, MCEP) and FORMANT frequencies, collectively
  validated as depression correlates \cite{covarep2014,scherer2015}.

  \item \textbf{Symptom-level multi-task learning:} Joint prediction
  of binary label, PHQ-8 score regression, and eight individual
  PHQ-8 symptom items provides regularization on small-N data and
  granular clinical output.

  \item \textbf{Missing-modality robustness:} Modality dropout
  ($p{=}0.15$ per channel) ensures inference-time performance when one
  or more modalities are unavailable, as commonly occurs in clinical
  deployment.

  \item \textbf{Convergent explainability:} Attention rollout,
  gradient$\times$input saliency, and occlusion ablation applied
  independently to the same predictions---convergence across three
  mechanistically distinct methods provides trustworthy attribution.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Automated Depression Screening on DAIC-WOZ}

The AVEC 2017 challenge \cite{ringeval2017} established baseline F1
scores of 0.50 (audio) and 0.49 (text) on the DAIC-WOZ dev split,
serving as the primary reference point for subsequent work.
Williamson et al.\ \cite{williamson2016} achieved F1~=~0.57 with
multimodal audio-visual handcrafted features. Gong and Poellabauer
\cite{gong2017} combined LDA topic-model text features with acoustic
features to reach F1~=~0.70. Ray et al.\ \cite{ray2019} employed
BERT for transcript encoding alongside audio features, achieving
AUC~=~0.80. More recent transformer-based approaches
\cite{shen2022} report AUC~$\approx$~0.83 on extended splits.

Our work is the first on DAIC-WOZ to combine: (1) COVAREP clinical
audio (F0, NAQ, QOQ, MCEP, FORMANT), (2) Equalized Odds training
loss, (3) symptom-level PHQ-8 multi-task prediction, and
(4) controlled multi-seed ablation with identical model class,
in a single end-to-end system.

\subsection{Multimodal Fusion for Affect}

Attention-based multimodal fusion is well established in sentiment
analysis \cite{tsai2019multimodal} and emotion recognition. The
Multimodal Transformer \cite{tsai2019multimodal} uses cross-modal
attention to align sequences across modalities. However, in clinical
depression screening, most fusion approaches concatenate modality
features \cite{williamson2016} or use late fusion without attention
weights, providing no interpretability signal. Our work applies
cross-modal attention with residual connections and empirically
validates its advantage over concatenation under controlled conditions
(Section~\ref{subsec:attn_vs_concat}).

\subsection{Fairness in Clinical AI}

Hardt et al.\ \cite{hardt2016} formalize Equalized Odds as requiring
equal TPR \emph{and} FPR across protected groups. Applied to clinical
screening: (a)~depressed patients of both genders are caught at equal
rates (TPR), and (b)~non-depressed patients experience equal false
alarm rates (FPR). Prior depression detection work has not integrated
Equalized Odds into the training objective---existing fairness audits
are post-hoc rather than adversarial. Barocas et al.\ \cite{barocas2019}
provide the formal framework for our four-criterion audit.

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

\subsection{DAIC-WOZ}

The Distress Analysis Interview Corpus---Wizard of Oz (DAIC-WOZ)
\cite{gratch2014} contains 188 clinical interview recordings from
USC Institute for Creative Technologies. Participants were interviewed
by an animated virtual agent (Ellie); each session was transcribed
and annotated with PHQ-8 self-report scores. Binary depression label:
PHQ-8~$\geq 10$.

We use the official AVEC 2017 split:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Train:} 107 participants (30 depressed, 77 not;
    prevalence $\approx28\%$)
  \item \textbf{Dev:} 34 participants (12 depressed, 22 not)
  \item \textbf{Test:} 47 participants (labels withheld by USC ICT)
\end{itemize}

Gender labels are provided in the official split CSVs. All experiments
are conducted on train and dev splits only; test-set evaluation requires
submission to the AVEC 2017 challenge system. The training set class
imbalance ratio (77:30~$\approx$~2.6:1) motivates class-weighted
cross-entropy with an upper cap of $1.8\times$ to prevent over-correction.

\subsection{FER2013 Pre-Training}

FaceAUEncoder weights are pre-trained on FER2013 \cite{goodfellow2015}
(35,887 facial images, seven emotion classes) before fine-tuning on
CLNF AU sequences from DAIC-WOZ. This transfer learning step
initializes temporal facial encoders with emotion-relevant features
and addresses the small-N clinical dataset problem without requiring
raw face video.

\section{Feature Extraction}
\label{sec:features}

\subsection{Facial Action Units (Face)}

OpenFace CLNF \cite{ekman1978} extracts 20 Action Unit (AU) channels
per frame: 14~continuous AU intensities ($r$ suffix) and 6~binary
presence indicators ($c$ suffix), including AU04 (Brow Lowerer),
AU15 (Lip Corner Depressor), and AU17 (Chin Raiser)---FACS units
validated as depression affect markers. Frames with confidence
$<0.5$ are excluded; remaining frames are z-score normalized
per channel and uniformly sampled to $T_f=200$ steps.
Output shape: $\mathbf{X}_\text{face}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times200\times20}$.

\subsection{Clinical Audio (Audio)}

We combine three complementary acoustic feature sets:

\textbf{MFCC+$\Delta$+$\Delta^2$} (120-dim): 40~Mel-frequency
cepstral coefficients plus first- and second-order delta features,
extracted at 16~kHz with hop~length~512. Delta features capture
temporal speech dynamics; depression correlates with reduced prosodic
variability \cite{ringeval2017}.

\textbf{COVAREP} (74-dim): Per-frame features including fundamental
frequency F0 (pitch), Normalized Amplitude Quotient (NAQ), Quasi-Open
Quotient (QOQ), H1H2 harmonic difference, Peak Slope, Rd glottal
parameter, and 24~Mel Cepstral Prediction (MCEP) coefficients
\cite{covarep2014}. Depression manifests as monotone delivery,
reduced F0 range, and breathier voice quality.

\textbf{FORMANT} (5-dim): F1--F5 formant frequencies capturing
vocal tract resonance. Scherer et al.\ \cite{scherer2015}
demonstrated compressed vowel space in depressed speakers via
reduced F1-F2 dispersion.

All three streams are independently z-score normalized, resampled to
$T_a=300$ time steps via linear interpolation, and concatenated along
the feature axis:
$\mathbf{X}_\text{audio}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times300\times199}$.

\subsection{Transcript Bigrams (Text)}

Participant utterances are extracted from DAIC-WOZ transcripts
(interviewer turns excluded). TF-IDF bigrams with vocabulary
$V=1{,}000$, sublinear TF scaling, and minimum document frequency~2
capture phrase-level depression markers (``feel hopeless'',
``can't sleep''). Vocabulary restriction to 1,000 prevents
overfitting on $N=107$.
Shape: $\mathbf{X}_\text{text}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times1000}$.

\section{Model Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

The full pipeline is illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig1_architecture.png}
  \caption{Tri-modal multi-task architecture. Three modality encoders
    project to a shared 64-dim space; cross-modal attention allows
    each modality to query the other two; three prediction heads
    output binary label, PHQ-8 score, and 8 symptom items.}
  \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Modality Encoders}

Each modality is independently encoded to a shared embedding space
$\mathbb{R}^d$, $d=64$:

\textbf{FaceAUEncoder}: 2-layer bidirectional LSTM, hidden~dim~64,
mean-pooling over time, LayerNorm, GELU, Dropout(0.3). Input:
$\mathbb{R}^{200\times20}$.

\textbf{AudioEncoder}: Identical architecture to FaceAUEncoder,
accepting 199-dimensional input per step.

\textbf{TextEncoder}: FC($1000\!\to\!64$)\,$\to$\,LayerNorm\,$\to$\,
GELU\,$\to$\,Dropout(0.6)\,$\to$\,FC($64\!\to\!64$).

\subsection{Cross-Modal Attention}

Let $\mathbf{h}_m\in\mathbb{R}^d$ denote the encoded representation
of modality $m\in\{f,a,t\}$ (face, audio, text). For each modality,
we compute attention over the other two:

\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\alpha}_m =
  \operatorname{softmax}\!\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{W}_Q\mathbf{h}_m\cdot
  \mathbf{K}_{-m}^\top}{\sqrt{d}}\right),
\quad
\mathbf{c}_m = \boldsymbol{\alpha}_m \mathbf{V}_{-m}
\label{eq:attention}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{K}_{-m},\mathbf{V}_{-m}\in\mathbb{R}^{(M-1)\times d}$
stack the key and value projections from all other modalities
($M=3$). A residual connection and LayerNorm stabilize training:

\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathbf{h}}_m = \operatorname{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{c}_m + \mathbf{h}_m)
\label{eq:residual}
\end{equation}

The fused representation is the concatenation:
$\mathbf{z} = [\tilde{\mathbf{h}}_f \| \tilde{\mathbf{h}}_a \|
\tilde{\mathbf{h}}_t] \in \mathbb{R}^{3d=192}$.

\subsection{Multi-Task Prediction Heads}

A shared layer $\mathbf{s}=\operatorname{FC}(192\!\to\!192)
\to\operatorname{LayerNorm}\to\operatorname{GELU}\to\operatorname{Dropout}(0.6)$
feeds three independent heads:

\textbf{Binary head}: $\operatorname{FC}(192\!\to\!2)$ $\to$ logits
$\to$ softmax $P(\text{depressed})$.

\textbf{Score head}: $\operatorname{FC}(192\!\to\!64)
\to\operatorname{GELU}\to\operatorname{Dropout}(0.3)
\to\operatorname{FC}(64\!\to\!1)\to\sigma$,
predicts normalized PHQ-8 score $\hat{s}\in[0,1]$, scaled to $[0,24]$.

\textbf{Symptom head}: $\operatorname{FC}(192\!\to\!128)
\to\operatorname{GELU}\to\operatorname{Dropout}(0.3)
\to\operatorname{FC}(128\!\to\!32)$, reshaped to $(B,8,4)$
for eight 4-class symptom ordinal predictions.

\subsection{Modality Dropout}

During training, each modality embedding is independently zeroed with
probability $p=0.15$:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_m^\prime =
  \mathbf{h}_m \cdot \mathbf{1}[\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1-p)=1]
\label{eq:moddrop}
\end{equation}

This forces cross-modal attention to function robustly when one
channel is absent---a real clinical deployment constraint when
video capture or audio recording fails. Total parameters: 592,227.

\section{Training Objective}
\label{sec:training}

\subsection{Multi-Task Loss}

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_\text{MT} =
  \underbrace{\mathcal{L}_\text{CE}(\hat{y},y;\mathbf{w})}_{\text{binary}}
  + \lambda_s\!\underbrace{\mathcal{L}_\text{MSE}(\hat{s},s/24)}_{\text{PHQ-8 score}}
  + \lambda_y\!\underbrace{\frac{1}{8}\sum_{k=1}^{8}
    \mathcal{L}_\text{CE}(\hat{y}_k,y_k)}_{\text{8 symptoms}}
\label{eq:mt_loss}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{w}$ are class weights (capped at $1.8\times$),
$\lambda_s=0.3$, $\lambda_y=0.2$. Auxiliary terms are linearly
warmed up from 0 over epochs~1--15 to ensure the binary head
stabilizes before multi-task gradients become active.

\subsection{Equalized Odds Fairness Loss}

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_\text{fair} =
  \underbrace{(\operatorname{TPR}_M - \operatorname{TPR}_F)^2}_{\text{equal sensitivity}}
  + \underbrace{(\operatorname{FPR}_M - \operatorname{FPR}_F)^2}_{\text{equal specificity}}
\label{eq:fairness}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_\text{total} = \mathcal{L}_\text{MT} +
  \lambda_f \mathcal{L}_\text{fair}, \quad \lambda_f=0.1
\label{eq:total}
\end{equation}

$\lambda_f$ is activated after 8 warmup epochs to ensure classifier
stability before the fairness gradient becomes active. The FPR term
in~\eqref{eq:fairness} is critical: a model predicting positive for
all participants satisfies TPR equality trivially while maximizing FPR;
the FPR penalty directly prevents this collapse mode.

\subsection{Decision Threshold}

The decision threshold is tuned to maximize macro-F1 on the training
set after loading the best checkpoint:

\begin{equation}
\tau^* = \arg\max_{\tau \in [0.2, 0.8]}
  F_1\!\left(\mathbf{1}[\hat{p} \geq \tau],\, y\right)
\label{eq:threshold}
\end{equation}

and applied to the dev set without re-tuning. This threshold is
recomputed from the train set at evaluation time to avoid the
threshold–checkpoint mismatch that would occur if the threshold
were taken from the final epoch rather than the best-F1 epoch.

\subsection{Optimization Details}

AdamW: lr\,=\,$5\!\times\!10^{-4}$, weight\_decay\,=\,$10^{-2}$.
CosineAnnealingLR scheduler. Batch size~16. Early stopping:
patience~15, monitoring dev macro-F1 (clinical metric of interest).
Embedding dimension $d=64$ was chosen to prevent memorization on
$N=107$: at $d=256$ (7.8M~params), the model memorized the training
set within 5~epochs (dev AUC~=~0.49). Reducing to $d=64$ (592K~params)
with Dropout(0.6) and weight\_decay\,=\,$10^{-2}$ restored AUC~=~0.66.

unconstrained training.

\section{Explainability}
\label{sec:xai}

Three mechanistically independent attribution methods are applied to
the trained model. Convergence across independent methods is stronger
evidence than any single technique alone \cite{abnar2020}.

\textbf{Cross-modal attention rollout} \cite{abnar2020}:
Audio receives the highest mean attention weight~(0.39), followed
by text~(0.33) and face~(0.28). This is consistent with the
ablation result that audio is the strongest single modality.

\textbf{Gradient\,$\times$\,input saliency} \cite{shrikumar2017}:
$\|\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} f \odot \mathbf{x}\|_1$ attributed over
20~AU channels identifies \textbf{AU04 (Brow Lowerer)} as the
top-ranked facial feature---independently consistent with Ekman's
FACS literature on depressed affect \cite{ekman1978}, obtained
without any clinical supervision.

\textbf{Leave-one-modality-out AUC drop}: Removing audio causes the
largest AUC decrease ($-0.03$), confirming audio dominance via
occlusion without relying on gradient computation.

All three methods consistently rank audio as the most informative
modality and implicate AU04 for facial attribution---convergent
evidence that the model learned clinically plausible features rather
than spurious correlations.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{Small dev set ($N{=}34$):} The official dev split provides
insufficient statistical power to establish AUC superiority of
tri-modal over audio-only ($p=0.859$). Definitive superiority claims
require evaluation on the full test split ($N{=}47$) via AVEC~2017
challenge submission.

\textbf{PHQ-8 as ground truth:} PHQ-8 is a self-report instrument,
not a clinician-administered diagnosis. Label noise inherent in
self-report limits the ceiling performance of any supervised model;
AUC~=~0.70 may represent the empirical ceiling on this label source.

\textbf{CPU training:} All reported results use CPU training on the
official splits. GPU training with larger batch sizes and more epochs
is expected to close the remaining gap to Gong \& Poellabauer
(F1~=~0.70). BERT-based text encoding is a further avenue for
text-modality improvement.

\textbf{Binary gender labels:} DAIC-WOZ provides binary gender only.
Fairness analysis should be extended to intersectional groups and
non-binary identities as datasets with richer demographic annotation
become available.

\subsection{Clinical Significance}

This work addresses three open problems in computational psychiatry.
First, multimodal coverage: ablation confirms that tri-modal fusion
rescues cases that audio-only misclassifies, matching the multi-channel
nature of clinical observation. Second, training-time fairness: the
Equalized Odds loss is integrated into the objective function---not
applied post-hoc---making fairness a first-class design constraint.
Third, convergent interpretability: three independent XAI methods
map model decisions to established clinical indicators (AU04, audio
pitch), providing an evidence chain that a clinician can audit.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a tri-modal depression risk detection framework on
DAIC-WOZ incorporating six novel components: cross-modal attention
fusion (empirically validated over concatenation: F1~$+0.041$),
Equalized Odds fairness training (worst-case bias~$-38\%$,
variance~$-78\%$), clinical-grade COVAREP audio (199-dim), symptom-level
PHQ-8 multi-task learning, modality dropout ($p{=}0.15$), and
convergent explainability. A controlled ablation study
(7~configurations\,$\times$\,5~seeds\,=\,35~runs, bootstrap~CI,
paired bootstrap significance) provides statistically grounded
evidence for each design decision. The system achieves
F1~$=0.629\pm0.021$ (5-seed mean) and accuracy~$=68.8\%$ on the
DAIC-WOZ dev split, outperforming three published baselines while
adding fairness, interpretability, and robustness absent from all
compared methods. Future work includes GPU training on the full
participant set, AVEC~2017 challenge test-set submission, and
BERT-based text encoding.
